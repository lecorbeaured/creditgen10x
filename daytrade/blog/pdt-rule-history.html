The PDT Rule: A 25-Year History of Keeping Retail Traders Out

The $25,000 rule wasn't always controversial. When FINRA introduced it in 2001 after the dot-com meltdown, it seemed like common sense regulation. Over 25 years, it became something else entirely.

1999-2000: The Dot-Com Retail Trading Explosion

The late 1990s saw the first mass retail trading boom. Online brokerages democratized market access, commissions fell from $30+ to $10, and millions of new traders flooded the markets. Many were trading on margin, making dozens of trades per week, and some were making extraordinary gains. Then the crash came. Between 2000 and 2002, the Nasdaq fell 78%. Retail margin accounts blew up across the country.

2001: NASD Rule 2520 Is Born

In response, FINRA's predecessor (the NASD) introduced the Pattern Day Trader rule in 2001, incorporated into NASD Rule 2520. The $25,000 minimum was chosen as a number significant enough to deter casual speculation while not completely excluding serious participants. At the time, it represented roughly 2-3 years of savings for median-income Americans.

2001-2020: The Rule Calcifies

For nearly two decades, the rule sat largely unchanged while the world around it transformed dramatically. Online trading commissions dropped to zero. Smartphones put a brokerage in every pocket. Crypto made 24/7 trading normal for a generation of investors. The $25,000 threshold — unchanged since 2001 — grew from a significant barrier to an enormous one as savings rates stagnated and wealth inequality widened.

2021-2023: Meme Stocks Crack the Narrative

The GameStop saga of early 2021 put retail trading in the national spotlight. Suddenly everyone was aware that millions of small investors were effectively second-class citizens in the market — limited in how they could trade by an arbitrary capital threshold. The PDT rule became a target. FINRA began receiving increasing pressure to modernize it.

2025-2026: The Proposal and Passage

FINRA proposed the overhaul in December 2025. The comment period generated overwhelming public support. The SEC approved the change on April 14, 2026 — 25 years after the rule was introduced.
