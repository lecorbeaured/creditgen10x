Margin Calls Explained: What Every New Day Trader Must Know

A margin call is the moment your broker decides you've run out of runway. Under the new real-time margin framework, that moment can arrive faster and with less warning than it did under the old fixed rules.

What Is a Margin Call?

A margin call occurs when your account equity falls below the margin required to maintain your open positions. Your broker demands you either deposit additional funds immediately or they liquidate your positions — at their discretion, at market price, on their timeline. You do not get to choose which positions are sold or at what price.

How the New System Changes Margin Calls

Under the old PDT system, margin calls were typically assessed at the end of the day. The new real-time framework means your broker monitors your margin requirement continuously. A fast-moving adverse position can trigger a margin call within minutes of your entry. This is not a notification followed by time to respond — some brokers will auto-liquidate positions instantly.

How to Avoid a Margin Call

  • Keep a cash buffer: Never use 100% of your available margin. Keep 30-40% of your buying power unused as a buffer against adverse moves.
  • Use hard stop losses: Set stops before entry on every trade. A position that never falls to your stop never triggers a margin call.
  • Avoid high-volatility stocks with thin margins: A stock that can move 20% in 10 minutes requires enormous margin per dollar of exposure.
  • Monitor your positions actively: Real-time margin monitoring requires real-time attention. Don't open positions and walk away.
  • Trade smaller: Position size is your primary defense against margin calls. If you can't afford a margin call, you can't afford that position size.

What to Do If You Get a Margin Call

If you receive a margin call notification: stop trading immediately, assess which positions to close to free up margin, act quickly — brokers with auto-liquidation will not wait, and do not deposit more money just to keep a losing position open. That is the most expensive mistake a trader can make.
