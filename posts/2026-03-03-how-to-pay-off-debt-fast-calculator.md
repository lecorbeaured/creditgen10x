The average American household carrying credit card debt owes over $6,000 — at an average APR above 20%. At that rate, making only minimum payments means paying thousands of dollars in interest and potentially taking a decade to become debt-free.

The good news: with the right strategy and even modest extra payments, you can dramatically cut both the timeline and the total cost. This guide breaks down exactly how to approach debt payoff, which method is right for your situation, and how to calculate your actual payoff date and total interest cost.

## Why Minimum Payments Are a Trap

Credit card companies are legally required to show you — on every statement — how long it will take to pay off your balance making only minimum payments. That number is often alarming: 15, 20, or even 30 years.

Minimum payments are calculated as a small percentage of your balance (typically 1–3%) plus the monthly interest charge. As your balance decreases, so does your minimum payment. This means you're treading water — a shrinking minimum keeps you in debt longer and funnels an enormous amount of money to the lender in interest.

**Example:** A $6,000 balance at 22% APR with a $150 minimum payment takes over 6 years to pay off and costs more than $3,800 in interest. Adding just $100 extra per month cuts the payoff time to under 2.5 years and reduces total interest by more than $2,400.

## The Two Proven Debt Payoff Strategies

### The Debt Snowball Method

**How it works:** List your debts from smallest balance to largest, regardless of interest rate. Pay the minimum on everything except the smallest debt — throw every extra dollar at that one. When it's paid off, roll its entire payment into the next smallest. Repeat.

**Why it works psychologically:** Paying off a whole account — even a small one — provides a tangible win. Research in behavioral economics shows that people are more likely to stick with a plan when they experience early success. The "snowball" name comes from the momentum that builds as each freed-up payment gets added to the next.

**Best for:** People who struggle to stay motivated, have many small accounts, or have tried and quit other debt plans before.

**The tradeoff:** Because it ignores interest rates, the snowball method often costs more in total interest compared to the avalanche.

### The Debt Avalanche Method

**How it works:** List your debts from highest interest rate to lowest, regardless of balance. Pay the minimum on everything except the highest-APR debt — direct every extra dollar there first. When it's paid off, move to the next highest APR.

**Why it saves money:** High-APR debt grows fastest. By eliminating it first, you stop the bleeding at the source. The math is straightforward: the less time high-interest debt exists, the less interest it generates.

**Best for:** People who are mathematically motivated, have high-APR credit card debt, or have a clear plan they're confident they'll follow through.

**The tradeoff:** If your highest-APR debt also has a large balance, you may go months without a visible "win." Some people lose momentum.

### Which One Should You Choose?

Here's an honest answer: **the best method is the one you'll actually complete.**

Research from the Harvard Business Review found that debt payoff success correlates more strongly with motivation and consistency than with optimal strategy selection. A person who uses the snowball method and stays committed will outperform someone who picks the mathematically superior avalanche method and quits after six months.

That said, if the balances and APRs are similar, or if you're highly disciplined, the avalanche method can save hundreds or thousands of dollars. Use our calculator to run both scenarios and see the difference in your specific situation.

## 5 Practical Ways to Find Extra Payment Money

The calculator is motivating. But finding the extra $50, $100, or $200 to accelerate payoff is where most people get stuck. Here are approaches that actually work.

**1. Apply windfalls immediately.** Tax refunds, bonuses, gifts, and side income often get absorbed into everyday spending if they hit a checking account. Set up a rule: any lump sum above a threshold goes directly to your highest-priority debt before you can spend it.

**2. Find and cut one recurring expense.** Most people have subscriptions they've forgotten about. Review your last two bank statements and identify charges you don't actively use. Even canceling two $15/month subscriptions frees $30 — and over a year, $360 in extra debt payments.

**3. Temporarily reduce retirement contributions.** This is controversial, but if you're carrying high-APR credit card debt (above 12–15%), you're likely paying more in interest than you're earning in investment returns. Temporarily reducing contributions to minimum employer-match levels while paying off high-APR debt can make mathematical sense. Resume full contributions once debt is cleared.

**4. Negotiate a lower APR.** Call your credit card company and ask for a lower interest rate. This works more often than people expect — especially if you've been a customer for a while and have a record of on-time payments. Even a 3-point reduction on a $5,000 balance saves $150/year.

**5. Balance transfer to a 0% APR card.** If your credit score is above 670, you may qualify for a balance transfer card with 0% APR for 12–21 months. During that window, every dollar of payment goes toward principal — not interest. Watch for transfer fees (typically 3–5% of the balance) and make sure you can pay it off before the promotional period ends.

## What Happens to Your Credit Score While Paying Off Debt

One question people often have: does paying off debt improve or hurt your credit score?

**Paying off credit cards:** Lowering your credit card balances reduces your utilization ratio, which is 30% of your FICO score. This is almost always a positive change. You'll typically see the improvement within one to two billing cycles after your balance drops.

**Paying off installment loans:** Paying off a car loan or personal loan has a smaller but still positive effect. The caveat is that closing the account removes a positive tradeline from your report — this is usually still a net positive, but the score impact is more nuanced than with revolving debt.

**Avoiding new hard inquiries:** While paying off debt, avoid applying for new credit unless necessary. Each application triggers a hard inquiry that temporarily dips your score — small, but worth avoiding when you're trying to improve.

## A Note on Debt That's Already in Collections

If some of your debt has already been sent to a collections agency, the payoff math and strategy changes. Collections can remain on your credit report for up to 7 years from the date of first delinquency — regardless of whether you pay them.

Before paying any collection account, consider:

- **Debt validation:** You have the right to request that the collector prove the debt is yours and the amount is accurate. Send a validation request by certified mail within 30 days of first contact.
- **Statute of limitations:** Depending on your state, old debt may be past the point where a collector can legally sue you. Use our **[Debt SOL Checker](/statute-of-limitations-checker.html)** to look up your state's limits.
- **Pay for delete:** Negotiate with the collector to remove the entry entirely from your credit report in exchange for payment. Get this in writing before paying.
- **Banned collectors:** Some debt collectors have been permanently banned by the FTC. Check our **[Banned Collectors List](/banned-debt-collectors.html)** before paying any unfamiliar agency.
