## How Credit Scores Are Calculated

The FICO scoring model — used by over 90% of top lenders when making credit decisions — calculates your score from five factors. Each is weighted differently, and understanding the weights tells you where to focus your energy.

### Factor 1: Payment History (35%)

This is the single biggest factor in your score. It answers one question: do you pay your bills on time?

Every on-time payment adds to a positive track record. Every late payment — even one that's just 30 days past due — can drop your score by 60 to 110 points depending on how strong your history was before it. Accounts sent to collections, charge-offs, and bankruptcies cause even larger drops that persist for years.

The good news: the damage from late payments fades over time. A 90-day late from four years ago hurts much less than a 30-day late from six months ago.

### Factor 2: Credit Utilization (30%)

Utilization is the percentage of your available revolving credit that you're using. If your credit card limit is $5,000 and your balance is $2,500, your utilization is 50% — which is too high.

FICO rewards low utilization. Scores above 740 typically carry utilization under 10%. The sweet spot is under 30% on every card and under 10% overall. Paying down balances is often the fastest way to improve your score — balances update monthly, so you can see results in 30–60 days.

**Key point:** utilization only applies to revolving credit (credit cards, lines of credit). Installment loans like mortgages and auto loans are not included in this calculation.

### Factor 3: Length of Credit History (15%)

This factor looks at three things: the age of your oldest account, the age of your newest account, and the average age of all your accounts. Older is better.

This is why closing old credit cards hurts your score — it removes account age from the calculation. If you want to stop using a card, consider keeping it open with a small recurring charge (like a streaming subscription) that you pay off monthly. This keeps the account active and preserves the age.

### Factor 4: Credit Mix (10%)

Lenders like to see that you can handle different types of credit responsibly. A mix of revolving credit (credit cards) and installment loans (auto, mortgage, student) scores better than credit cards alone.

This doesn't mean you should take out a loan just to improve your mix — the hard inquiry and new debt aren't worth it. But if you're considering a credit builder loan for other reasons, the mix benefit is a real bonus.

### Factor 5: New Credit and Hard Inquiries (10%)

Every time you apply for credit — a card, a loan, a lease — the lender does a "hard inquiry" that temporarily drops your score by 3 to 10 points. Multiple applications in a short window signal financial stress to scoring models.

The effects are small and fade quickly. Most hard inquiries stop significantly impacting your score after 12 months and fall off your report entirely after 24 months.

## What Your Score Range Means in Practice

Understanding your number is only useful if you know what it unlocks. Here's what each tier means for real-world credit decisions.

**800–850 (Exceptional):** You'll qualify for the best interest rates on every product. Lenders compete for your business. Mortgage rates, auto loan APRs, and card APRs will be at their lowest possible tier.

**740–799 (Very Good):** You're in excellent shape. You'll be approved for nearly all products. Your rates may be slightly higher than the top tier but still very competitive.

**670–739 (Good):** This is where most approvals happen. You'll be approved for most cards and loans, though you won't get the absolute best rates. This is a good baseline for homebuyers — many mortgage programs require a minimum of 620–640.

**580–669 (Fair):** You're in the grey zone. Some lenders will approve you with higher APRs and stricter terms. Subprime auto loans and secured cards are common at this level. Improving your score before major purchases will save you significant money.

**Below 580 (Poor):** Approval for traditional credit products is limited. Secured cards, credit builder loans, and becoming an authorized user on a trusted family member's account are the primary paths forward.

## The 5 Fastest Ways to Improve Your Score

Not all credit improvements are equal. Some strategies produce results in 30 days. Others take years. Here's what actually moves the needle fastest.

**1. Pay down revolving balances.** If your utilization is above 30%, this is your fastest lever. Even paying one card down to near-zero can produce a meaningful improvement in the next billing cycle.

**2. Dispute inaccurate negative items.** If something on your report is wrong — a collection that isn't yours, a late payment that was reported in error, an account balance that's outdated — disputing and removing it can produce the single largest score jump. Always dispute by certified mail, not online, to protect your FCRA rights.

**3. Get added as an authorized user.** If a family member or close friend has a card with a long history, high limit, and low balance, being added as an authorized user can add that positive history to your report within 30–60 days.

**4. Set up autopay for minimums.** One missed payment can undo years of progress. Set up autopay for at least the minimum on every account so you never accidentally miss a due date.

**5. Keep old accounts open.** Closing a credit card you no longer use removes its credit limit from your utilization calculation and can reduce your average account age — both of which hurt your score.
