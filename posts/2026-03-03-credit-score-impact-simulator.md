Every credit decision you make — every application, payment, and account change — has a measurable impact on your credit score. But most people have no idea what those impacts actually are. They avoid opening new credit out of vague fear, or pay off a collection without understanding whether it will actually help them, or miss a payment and wonder how bad the damage really is.

This guide gives you the actual numbers: what happens to your score when you take specific actions, why each one works the way it does, and what to do (or not do) based on your situation.

## The Actions That Hurt Your Score

### Missing a Payment (30 Days Late)

**Score impact: −60 to −110 points**

This is the single most damaging common credit event. Payment history makes up 35% of your FICO score — the largest single factor — and a missed payment is direct, unambiguous evidence against it.

The impact is counterintuitively larger for people with higher scores. If your score is 780, a single 30-day late can drop you to 670–700. If your score is 600, the drop might only be 40–50 points because the scoring model already accounts for some payment risk.

Why such a large impact? Because lenders use credit scores to predict the probability of future defaults. Someone with a perfect payment history suddenly missing a payment sends a much stronger signal than someone with an already-imperfect record.

**What to do if this happens:**
- Pay the bill immediately, even though it's technically late
- Call the lender and request a "goodwill adjustment" — ask them to remove the late from your report. Many lenders will do this once for a customer with an otherwise clean history
- If it's your first late, your chances of a successful goodwill request are high
- The mark stays on your report for 7 years, but its scoring weight diminishes significantly after 2 years of positive history following it

### Opening a New Credit Card

**Score impact: −5 to −10 points (temporary)**

Opening a new card triggers two negative effects: a hard inquiry (3–5 points) and a reduction in average account age. Both are temporary.

The hard inquiry impact fades after 12 months and disappears after 24. The average age impact gradually neutralizes as the new account ages alongside your other accounts.

**The flip side:** Over time, the new card actually helps your score. The additional available credit limit lowers your overall utilization ratio — a 30% factor in your score. A new card with a $5,000 limit on a profile with $15,000 in existing available credit doesn't add much. But on a profile with $3,000 in existing available credit, that $5,000 limit can meaningfully reduce overall utilization.

**Bottom line:** Don't open new cards frivolously, but don't fear them either. The long-term utilization benefit often outweighs the short-term dip.

### Closing an Old Credit Card

**Score impact: −5 to −25 points**

Closing a card removes its credit limit from your available credit, which raises your utilization ratio. It may also shorten your average account age if it's one of your older accounts. Both effects are negative.

The magnitude depends on the card's limit relative to your total available credit and how old the account is.

**Example:** You have four cards with total limits of $20,000 and balances of $4,000 (20% utilization). You close one card with a $5,000 limit. Now you have $15,000 in available credit and the same $4,000 in balances — your utilization jumps to 27%. That jump alone can cost 10–20 points.

**What to do instead:** If you want to stop using a card (or avoid an annual fee), ask the issuer for a product change — downgrading to a no-fee version of the same card. This keeps the account history and available credit intact without the fee.

### A Hard Inquiry (Loan or Card Application)

**Score impact: −3 to −10 points (temporary)**

Hard inquiries are small, temporary, and often worth it. Don't let fear of a 5-point dip stop you from applying for a card or loan that genuinely improves your financial situation.

**The rate-shopping exception:** If you're shopping for a mortgage, auto loan, or student loan — and submit multiple applications within a 14–45 day window — the scoring models count them as a single inquiry. They understand you're comparing rates, not opening multiple lines of credit.

### Filing for Bankruptcy

**Score impact: −130 to −200 points**

Bankruptcy produces one of the largest single-event score drops possible. Chapter 7 stays on your report for 10 years. Chapter 13 stays for 7.

However — and this is important — many people's scores *begin recovering within 1–2 years* of filing. Bankruptcy clears existing debts, stopping the ongoing damage from delinquent accounts. Starting fresh, with a single positive tradeline and consistent on-time payments, often produces faster recovery than slowly sinking under unmanageable debt.

If you're considering bankruptcy, consult a bankruptcy attorney before deciding. Many offer free consultations. The credit impact is significant but not permanent, and it's sometimes the right financial decision.

## The Actions That Help Your Score

### Paying Off a Credit Card

**Score impact: +20 to +50 points**

Paying off a credit card lowers your utilization ratio — 30% of your score — and the improvement shows up within one to two billing cycles. This is one of the fastest, most predictable score improvements available.

The impact is largest when:
- The card was at high utilization (above 50%)
- The card represents a significant portion of your total available credit
- Your overall utilization drops into a lower tier (from above 30% to below 30%, or from above 10% to below 10%)

**Keep the card open.** After paying it off, don't close it. An open card with a $0 balance adds maximum available credit and keeps your utilization low.

### Paying Off a Collection Account

**Score impact: 0 to +80 points (varies significantly by scoring model)**

This is the most nuanced item on the list. The impact of paying a collection depends heavily on which scoring model your lender uses.

**Under FICO 8** (the most widely used model for credit decisions): Paid and unpaid collections are weighted similarly. Paying a collection may produce little to no score improvement under this model.

**Under FICO 9 and VantageScore 4.0**: Paid collections are ignored entirely. If your lender uses one of these newer models — which is increasingly common — paying the collection can produce a significant improvement.

**The strategic move:** Before paying any collection, negotiate "pay for delete" in writing. A pay-for-delete agreement means the collector removes the entry entirely from your report in exchange for payment. Under every scoring model, a deleted entry is better than a paid entry. Get it in writing before you pay.

Also verify: is this collector even legitimate? Check our **[FTC Banned Collectors List](/banned-debt-collectors.html)** before paying any unfamiliar agency.

### Successfully Disputing an Error

**Score impact: +10 to +100 points**

Removing an inaccurate negative item from your credit report can produce the largest single score improvement available — sometimes more than any other action.

The exact impact depends on what was removed:
- A paid collection that wasn't yours, removed entirely: potentially 50–100 points
- A late payment reported incorrectly: 30–60 points depending on how recent
- An outdated balance that should have been updated: 10–30 points

The FCRA gives you the right to dispute any inaccurate item with all three bureaus. Always dispute by certified mail — not online portals — to create a legal paper trail and protect your right to follow up if the bureau doesn't respond within 30 days.
