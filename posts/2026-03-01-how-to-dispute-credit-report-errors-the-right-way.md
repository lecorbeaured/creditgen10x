Millions of Americans have errors on their credit reports. The Federal Trade Commission estimates that 1 in 5 consumers has at least one error — and 1 in 20 has an error significant enough to affect their credit score. If you've spotted something wrong on your report, disputing it is your legal right under the Fair Credit Reporting Act (FCRA). But most disputes fail — not because the error isn't real, but because people skip the preparation steps that protect their rights.

This guide walks you through exactly how to dispute credit report errors correctly, what to avoid, and what to do when the bureaus push back.

## Before You Dispute: 5 Steps That Protect You

Most people jump straight to writing a dispute letter. That's the number one mistake. Before you dispute anything, complete these steps in order.

### 1. Freeze Your Secondary Bureaus

Everyone knows Equifax, Experian, and TransUnion. But creditors also check smaller bureaus like **Innovis**, **ChexSystems**, **LexisNexis**, and **SageStream** during the dispute verification process. If these bureaus have conflicting information, your dispute can be rejected.

Freeze all four — it's free and takes about 5 minutes per bureau.

### 2. Document Everything Before You Start

Screenshot every page of all three credit reports. Save them as PDFs with the date in the filename — for example, `Equifax_2026-03-01.pdf`. Create a spreadsheet tracking every account you're disputing, which bureau it's on, and when you sent the letter.

**If you don't document it, it didn't happen.** Credit bureaus regularly claim they never received disputes. Your paper trail is your proof.

### 3. Never Dispute Online

Every major bureau website has a convenient "Dispute Online" button. Don't click it. When you submit an online dispute, you agree to terms that waive your rights under the FCRA — including your right to sue if the bureau mishandles your dispute.

Online disputes are also processed by algorithms, not humans. They have character limits that prevent you from fully explaining complex errors, and they make it nearly impossible to attach supporting documentation.

### 4. Never Dispute by Phone

Same principle applies. Phone conversations are not legally binding. The bureau or creditor can deny the conversation ever happened. Everything must be in writing.

### 5. Buy Certified Mail Supplies

Every dispute letter must go out via **Certified Mail with Return Receipt Requested**. The green card that comes back to you is legal proof they received your dispute and starts the 30-day investigation clock by law.

## How to Write an Effective Dispute Letter

Your dispute letter doesn't need to be long or complicated. It needs to be specific, factual, and sent to the right address.

**What to include:**

- Your full name, current address, date of birth, and the last 4 digits of your SSN
- The exact account name and account number you're disputing
- A clear, specific reason for the dispute (not "this isn't mine" — be specific)
- A request for the item to be investigated and corrected or removed
- A list of enclosed supporting documents

**What NOT to include:**

- Emotional language or accusations
- Multiple disputed items in one letter (dispute 1–3 items per letter per bureau)
- Your full Social Security Number

Always sign with **blue ink** — not black. Blue ink proves your letter is an original document, not a photocopy.

## What Happens After You Send Your Dispute

1. **The bureau has 30 days to investigate** (45 if you provide new information during the process)
2. They must contact the original data furnisher (the creditor or collection agency) to verify the information
3. You receive a written response with the results
4. If the item is corrected or removed, the bureau must notify the other two bureaus of the change
5. If they verify it as accurate and you disagree — escalate

## If Your Dispute Is Rejected: What to Do Next

A rejection isn't the end. Here's your escalation path:

**Step 1 — Dispute with the original creditor directly.** The FCRA gives you the right to dispute inaccurate information directly with the company that reported it, not just the bureau.

**Step 2 — File a CFPB complaint.** Go to consumerfinance.gov/complaint. The CFPB has authority over credit bureaus and creditors. A formal complaint often triggers a real human review — not an automated one.

**Step 3 — File an FTC complaint.** At reportfraud.ftc.gov. This creates a paper trail that can be used as evidence if you later pursue legal action.

**Step 4 — Consult a consumer law attorney.** Under the FCRA, if a bureau willfully fails to correct a verified error, you may be entitled to actual damages, statutory damages, and attorney's fees. Many consumer attorneys work on contingency — meaning they don't get paid unless you win.

## Common Dispute Mistakes That Get Letters Rejected

- **Disputing everything at once** — flags your account as frivolous
- **Using a template without customizing it** — bureaus recognize boilerplate letters
- **Not including your identifying information** — letters get returned unprocessed
- **Missing the certified mail step** — no proof of delivery, no legal timestamp
- **Calling to follow up instead of writing** — verbal follow-ups don't count

---

The dispute process works when you follow it correctly. The FCRA gives you powerful rights — but only if you exercise them the right way.
