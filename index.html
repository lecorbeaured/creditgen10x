What Is a Collection? (And Why It Matters)

A collection is a debt that the original creditor gave up on collecting and sold to a third-party debt collector. When you see "collection" or "charged-off" on your credit report, it means:

  • The original creditor (credit card company, hospital, etc.) wrote off your debt as a loss
  • They sold it to a collection agency for pennies on the dollar
  • The collection agency now owns the right to collect from you
  • This negative mark stays on your credit report for 7 years from the first missed payment

⚠️ Critical Warning

Paying a collection does NOT remove it from your credit report. It simply changes the status to "paid collection"—which still hurts your credit. Never pay without a "pay for delete" agreement in writing first.

How Collections Affect Your Credit

A single collection can significantly damage your credit, depending on your current standing. The newer the collection, the bigger the impact. Collections under 2 years old hurt the most.

How to Read a Credit Report (Section by Section)

1. Personal Information

Check for errors in your name, address, employer, or SSN. If you see an address you've never lived at, that's a red flag for identity theft or mixed files (your info merged with someone else's).

2. Accounts (Tradelines)

Each account shows:

  • Account Name: Creditor or lender
  • Account Number: Usually partially masked
  • Date Opened: When the account was established
  • Balance: Current amount owed
  • Status: Open, closed, charged-off, in collections
  • Payment History: 24-48 months of on-time vs. late payments

💡 Pro Tip

Look for "30," "60," "90" in the payment history. These indicate late payments. A "CO" means charged-off (sent to collections). "CLS" means closed.

3. Collections & Public Records

This is where collections, bankruptcies, liens, and judgments appear. Check the date of first delinquency—this determines when the item falls off (7 years later).

4. Credit Inquiries

Hard inquiries (from credit applications) may affect your credit. Soft inquiries (checking your own report, pre-approvals) do not.

Common Credit Report Errors to Look For

Accounts That Aren't Yours

Look for accounts you never opened. This could be identity theft, or a "mixed file" where your credit history got merged with someone who has a similar name or SSN.

Incorrect Account Status

Duplicate Entries

Outdated Negative Items

Incorrect Balances or Limits

High balances hurt your credit utilization ratio. Make sure your credit limits and current balances are accurate.

How to Build Credit From Scratch: The Complete Step-by-Step Guide

If you have no credit history, lenders see a blank file — and a blank file is almost as damaging as a bad one. The good news: building credit from scratch is entirely possible, and you can have a measurable score within 3 to 6 months by following the right steps in the right order.

Why Building Credit From Scratch Is Different

When you have no credit history, you're considered "credit invisible." According to the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau, roughly 26 million Americans are in this situation. Traditional lenders won't approve you because you have no track record — but you need a track record to get approved. This catch-22 is solvable with the right tools.

Step 1: Open a Secured Credit Card

A secured credit card requires a refundable deposit — usually $200 to $500 — that becomes your credit limit. Because the lender's risk is covered by your deposit, approval is almost guaranteed even with no credit history.

What to look for in a secured card:

  • No annual fee or a low annual fee (under $35)
  • Reports to all three major bureaus (Equifax, Experian, TransUnion)
  • Offers an upgrade path to an unsecured card after 12 months
  • Doesn't require a credit check for approval

Use the card for one small recurring purchase per month — like a streaming subscription. Pay the full balance before the due date every single month. This builds a perfect payment history, which is the single most important factor in your credit score (35%).

Step 2: Add a Credit-Builder Loan

Credit-builder loans are specifically designed for people building credit from scratch. Unlike a regular loan, the money you borrow is held in a savings account while you make monthly payments. At the end of the term, you receive the funds plus the positive payment history on your credit report.

Many credit unions and online lenders like Self offer credit-builder loans with no credit check required. Adding one creates a mix of credit types — another factor that helps your score.

Step 3: Become an Authorized User

Ask a family member or trusted friend with good credit to add you as an authorized user on one of their older credit card accounts. You don't need to use or even receive the card. The account's history — including its age and payment record — gets added to your credit file immediately.

This is one of the fastest ways to build credit from scratch because you inherit years of positive history overnight.

Step 4: Use a Credit-Building Service

Services like Kikoff add a credit account to your report with no credit check, no interest, and no hard inquiry. Kikoff's $750 credit line reports to the bureaus monthly, boosting your credit utilization ratio and adding positive payment history — two of the most impactful score factors.

How Long Does It Take to Build Credit From Scratch?

Most people can reach a 650+ score within 6 months of opening their first account and paying on time. A score above 700 typically takes 12 to 18 months of consistent on-time payments and low utilization. The longer your accounts stay open and in good standing, the higher your score climbs.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I build credit with no money?

Yes. Becoming an authorized user on someone else's account costs nothing. Some credit-builder services like Kikoff start at $5/month, which is lower than most secured card fees.

Does a debit card build credit?

No. Debit card activity is never reported to credit bureaus. Only credit accounts — credit cards, loans, and lines of credit — build your score.

How do I build credit at 18?

Start with a secured credit card or become an authorized user on a parent's account. Both require no prior credit history and can establish your file within 30 to 60 days.

What credit score do you start with?

You don't start with a score. You have no score until you have at least one account that's been open for six months and has been reported to the bureaus within the last six months.

How to Read a Credit Report: Every Section Explained

Your credit report is a 10 to 30 page document that determines whether you get approved for loans, apartments, and sometimes even jobs. Most people never read it — and that's exactly why errors go undetected for years. This guide breaks down every section so you can read yours with confidence.

What Is a Credit Report?

A credit report is a detailed record of your borrowing history compiled by one of the three major credit bureaus: Equifax, Experian, or TransUnion. Each bureau maintains its own file, so you actually have three separate credit reports that may contain different information.

Your credit report is NOT the same as your credit score. The report is the raw data. The score is a number calculated from that data using a specific formula (like FICO or VantageScore).

Section 1: Personal Information

This section contains your identifying information: full name, current and previous addresses, date of birth, Social Security Number (partially masked), phone numbers, and employer history.

What to check: Look for addresses you've never lived at, misspelled names, or incorrect Social Security Numbers. These errors can indicate identity theft or a "mixed file" — where your credit history has been accidentally merged with someone else's.

Section 2: Account Summary (Tradelines)

This is the largest section and the most important. It lists every credit account you have or have had, including credit cards, auto loans, mortgages, student loans, and personal loans.

For each account, you'll see:

  • Creditor name — the lender or company
  • Account number — usually partially masked for security
  • Account type — revolving (credit cards) or installment (loans)
  • Date opened — when the account was established
  • Credit limit or original loan amount
  • Current balance
  • Payment status — current, 30 days late, 60 days late, charged-off
  • Payment history grid — a month-by-month record, usually 24 months

Understanding Payment History Codes

The payment history grid uses codes. Here's what they mean: OK or ✓ means paid on time. 30, 60, 90, 120 mean days late. CO means charged-off (sent to collections). CLS means the account is closed. R before a number means revolving account status.

Section 3: Collections & Public Records

Collections appear when an original creditor sells your debt to a collection agency. Public records include bankruptcies, civil judgments, and tax liens.

Critical detail — the Date of First Delinquency (DOFD): This is the date you first missed a payment on the original account. This date — not when it was sold to collections — determines when the item must be removed from your report (7 years from the DOFD). Always verify this date is accurate.

Section 4: Credit Inquiries

Every time someone pulls your credit, it's recorded as an inquiry. There are two types:

  • Hard inquiries — triggered by credit applications (loans, cards). These can lower your score by a few points and stay on your report for 2 years.
  • Soft inquiries — triggered by pre-approval checks, employer background checks, or you checking your own credit. These never affect your score.

Section 5: Consumer Statements

You can add a 100-word personal statement to your credit report explaining a dispute or extenuating circumstance. Lenders rarely read these, but they're useful during an active dispute process.

Common Errors to Look For

  • Accounts you never opened (possible identity theft or mixed file)
  • Incorrect account status — paid accounts still showing as past due
  • Duplicate entries — same debt listed twice
  • Wrong balance or credit limit (affects your utilization ratio)
  • Negative items past the 7-year removal date
  • "Closed by creditor" on accounts you voluntarily closed

Frequently Asked Questions

How many credit reports do I have?

You have three — one from Equifax, one from Experian, and one from TransUnion. They often contain different information because not all creditors report to all three bureaus.

What does "charge-off" mean on a credit report?

A charge-off means the original creditor wrote your debt off as a loss after 120-180 days of non-payment. The debt still exists and can be collected — it's just been reclassified on their books. It's one of the most damaging entries on a credit report.

How long do negative items stay on my credit report?

Most negative items — late payments, collections, charge-offs — stay for 7 years from the date of first delinquency. Chapter 7 bankruptcy stays for 10 years. Hard inquiries stay for 2 years.

What is a good credit report?

A good credit report shows no negative items, a low utilization ratio (under 30%), a long average account age, a mix of account types, and few recent hard inquiries.

How to Get a Credit Report for Free (Official & Safe Methods Only)

Getting your credit report for free is your legal right — guaranteed by federal law under the Fair Credit Reporting Act (FCRA). But most people don't know where to go, and dozens of copycat websites are designed to trick you into paying for something you're entitled to for free. Here's exactly where to go and what to avoid.

The Only Official Free Source: AnnualCreditReport.com

The one legitimate, government-authorized website to get free credit reports is AnnualCreditReport.com. This site was created jointly by Equifax, Experian, and TransUnion in compliance with federal law. It gives you access to your report from all three bureaus.

How to use AnnualCreditReport.com:

  1. Go to AnnualCreditReport.com — type it directly, don't search for it
  2. Click "Request your free credit reports"
  3. Enter your name, address, Social Security Number, and date of birth
  4. Select which bureau(s) you want — you can request all three at once
  5. Answer identity verification questions from each bureau
  6. View and download your reports as PDFs

How often can you get them free? As of 2023, you can get your free credit reports from all three bureaus once per week at AnnualCreditReport.com — permanently, not just during the pandemic period. This is a significant upgrade from the previous once-per-year limit.

Free Reports Directly From Each Bureau

Each bureau also offers free access through their own websites. These are legitimate but sometimes limited:

  • Equifax — equifax.com offers one free report per year plus ongoing monitoring through myEquifax
  • Experian — experian.com offers a free account with monthly credit report access
  • TransUnion — transunion.com offers a free account with ongoing report access

Free Credit Monitoring Tools (Not Full Reports)

These tools give you an ongoing view of your credit — useful for monitoring, but they don't replace the full report for dispute purposes:

Does getting my free credit report hurt my score?

No. Checking your own credit report is a soft inquiry and has zero impact on your credit score, no matter how many times you do it.

Is AnnualCreditReport.com safe to enter my SSN?

How to Check Your Credit Report Without Hurting Your Score

One of the most common credit myths is that checking your own credit report hurts your score. It doesn't — and avoiding your report out of fear is one of the costliest mistakes you can make. Here's everything you need to know about checking your credit the right way.

Does Checking Your Credit Report Lower Your Score?

No — when you check your own credit report, it's recorded as a soft inquiry, which has zero impact on your credit score. The only inquiries that affect your score are hard inquiries, which occur when a lender pulls your report because you applied for credit.

You can check your credit report every day for a year and your score will not drop by a single point. This myth persists because people confuse checking their own report with a lender running a hard inquiry.

Hard vs. Soft Inquiries: The Key Difference

  • Soft inquiries (don't affect score): You checking your own report, pre-approval screenings, employer background checks, insurance company checks, account reviews by existing creditors
  • Hard inquiries (can lower score): Applying for a credit card, applying for a loan, applying to rent an apartment (in some cases), requesting a credit limit increase

A single hard inquiry typically lowers your score by fewer than 5 points and disappears from your report after 2 years. Multiple hard inquiries in a short window (rate shopping for a mortgage or auto loan) are treated as a single inquiry by most scoring models if they occur within a 14 to 45 day window.

How to Check Your Credit Report: Step by Step

  1. Go to AnnualCreditReport.com — the only official free source
  2. Click "Request your free credit reports"
  3. Enter your personal information (name, SSN, address, DOB)
  4. Select all three bureaus — Equifax, Experian, TransUnion
  5. Complete identity verification for each bureau
  6. Download each report as a PDF and save with the date in the filename

Best Free Tools to Monitor Your Credit Year-Round

For ongoing monitoring between your full report checks, these free tools alert you to changes in real time:

  • Credit Karma — free weekly TransUnion and Equifax scores, alerts for new accounts, inquiries, and balance changes
  • Experian Free — free monthly Experian report with dark web monitoring and FICO score
  • myEquifax — free Equifax report access plus Equifax credit score
  • Chase Credit Journey — free VantageScore 3.0, available even without a Chase account

How Often Should You Check Your Credit Report?

At minimum, check each of your three credit reports once per year. If you're actively repairing credit, dispute errors, or suspect identity theft, check monthly. A good strategy is to stagger your free reports — pull Equifax in January, Experian in May, TransUnion in September — so you have coverage throughout the year.

If you're preparing to apply for a mortgage, auto loan, or any major credit product, check all three reports at least 3 to 6 months in advance. This gives you time to dispute errors and see improvements reflected before a lender pulls your report.

What Is Identity Theft?

Identity theft happens when someone uses your personal information — your name, Social Security Number, date of birth, credit card numbers, or other identifying data — without your permission, usually to commit fraud or steal money.

Common types of identity theft include:

  • Financial identity theft — Opening credit cards or loans in your name
  • Tax identity theft — Filing a fraudulent tax return using your SSN to steal your refund
  • Medical identity theft — Using your insurance to receive medical care
  • Social Security identity theft — Claiming Social Security benefits under your number
  • Synthetic identity theft — Combining your real SSN with a fake name to create a new identity
  • Child identity theft — Using a minor's SSN, which often goes undetected for years

10 Warning Signs Your Identity Has Been Stolen (And What to Do Immediately)

Most identity theft victims don't realize what's happened until months — sometimes years — after the theft occurred. By then the damage is deep: fraudulent accounts in collections, a destroyed credit score, and a recovery process that can take years. Knowing the warning signs early is the single most effective way to limit the damage. Here are the 10 red flags that your identity may have already been compromised.

1. Unfamiliar Accounts on Your Credit Report

This is the most direct sign. If you pull your credit report and see a credit card, loan, or collection account you don't recognize, someone may have used your identity to open it. Even a single account you don't recognize warrants immediate investigation.

What to do: Document it, freeze your credit at all three bureaus immediately, and file an identity theft report at IdentityTheft.gov before disputing the account.

2. Hard Inquiries You Don't Recognize

Hard inquiries appear on your credit report every time someone applies for credit in your name. If you see inquiries from lenders you never contacted — especially multiple in a short window — someone may be applying for credit using your identity.

What to do: Contact each lender associated with the unknown inquiry to report fraud and request the application be flagged and closed.

3. Bills or Collection Calls for Accounts You Never Opened

Receiving a bill, statement, or debt collection call for an account you have no memory of is a major red flag. Thieves often use stolen identities to open accounts, max them out, and then disappear — leaving you with the collections.

What to do: Never confirm or deny the debt over the phone. Request everything in writing. Then check your credit report to see if the account appears there.

4. Your Credit Score Drops Suddenly With No Explanation

If your credit score drops significantly and you haven't missed any payments or opened new accounts, the cause may be fraudulent activity. High balances on accounts you didn't open, new derogatory marks, or a wave of hard inquiries can all tank your score without your knowledge.

What to do: Pull all three of your credit reports immediately and compare them line by line looking for accounts, inquiries, or address changes you don't recognize.

5. You're Denied Credit Unexpectedly

Being denied for a loan or credit card when you believe your credit is in good standing is a serious warning sign. Lenders reject applications when they see derogatory marks, high utilization, or collection accounts — all of which can appear on your report without your knowledge if someone is using your identity.

What to do: You're entitled to a free copy of the credit report used in the denial within 60 days. Request it and examine it carefully.

6. IRS Notice of a Duplicate Tax Return

Tax identity theft happens when a thief files a fraudulent return using your SSN to claim your refund before you file. The IRS will notify you if a return has already been filed under your SSN. This is one of the hardest forms of identity theft to resolve and can delay your legitimate refund by a year or more.

What to do: Respond to the IRS notice immediately, complete IRS Form 14039 (Identity Theft Affidavit), and contact the IRS Identity Protection Specialized Unit.

7. Medical Bills for Treatment You Never Received

Medical identity theft is one of the most dangerous and overlooked forms of identity theft. Thieves use your insurance information to receive medical treatment, leaving you with bills, incorrect medical records, and potential insurance coverage problems. This can have life-threatening consequences if fraudulent medical information gets mixed into your real records.

What to do: Request your medical records and Explanation of Benefits (EOB) statements from your insurer and review them for services you never received.

8. Mail Stops Arriving or You Receive Others' Mail

If your expected mail suddenly stops — statements, bills, tax documents — it could mean someone submitted a mail forwarding request in your name to intercept your financial correspondence. Receiving mail addressed to unfamiliar names at your address can mean your address is being used fraudulently.

What to do: Contact USPS to check for any change-of-address requests filed in your name. Set up USPS Informed Delivery for a daily digital preview of incoming mail.

9. Unfamiliar Addresses on Your Credit Report

The personal information section of your credit report lists all addresses associated with your credit file. If you see addresses you've never lived at — especially in other cities or states — it could indicate someone is using your identity in another location, or that your file has been mixed with someone else's.

10. Data Breach Notification

Receiving a letter or email that your information was exposed in a data breach doesn't mean identity theft has happened yet — but it means your data is now circulating on the dark web. Stolen data from breaches is often sold and used months or years after the breach itself.

What to do: Take breach notifications seriously. Freeze your credit, change affected passwords, and consider enrolling in identity theft monitoring immediately — before the data is used against you.

What to Do If Your Identity Has Been Stolen: Immediate Steps

  1. Place a fraud alert — Contact one of the three bureaus (they notify the others). A fraud alert requires lenders to take extra steps to verify identity before extending credit.
  2. Freeze your credit — Freeze all three bureaus plus secondary bureaus (Innovis, ChexSystems, LexisNexis). A freeze is the strongest protection available.
  3. File an identity theft report — Go to IdentityTheft.gov (FTC) and create a personal recovery plan. This generates a legal affidavit you'll need for disputes.
  4. Report to local police — Get a police report number. Some creditors and agencies require it during the recovery process.
  5. Dispute fraudulent accounts — Use certified mail only. Include your FTC Identity Theft Report with each dispute letter.
  6. Contact the IRS — If you suspect tax identity theft, proactively contact the IRS before filing season.
  7. Notify your bank and creditors — Alert your existing financial institutions so they can flag your accounts for suspicious activity.
